CHAPTER 5 – SQUARES AND SQUARE ROOTS

Introduction

You have already learned about square numbers in earlier classes. A number obtained by multiplying an integer by itself is called a perfect square.

Examples: \( 2 \times 2 = 4 \), \( 5 \times 5 = 25 \), \( 10 \times 10 = 100 \). Hence, 4, 25, and 100 are perfect squares.

The reverse process of finding which number squared gives a particular number is called finding the square root.

Example: Since \( 12 \times 12 = 144 \), we say \( \sqrt{144} = 12 \).

Properties of Square Numbers
  • The square of an even number is even.
  • The square of an odd number is odd.
  • Squares of numbers end with digits 0, 1, 4, 5, 6, or 9 — but never with 2, 3, 7, or 8.
  • There are exactly \( n \) perfect squares between \( n^2 \) and \( (n+1)^2 \).
Example Problem

Find whether 49 is a perfect square.

We know \( 7 \times 7 = 49 \). Hence, 49 is a perfect square and \( \sqrt{49} = 7 \).

Practice Question
